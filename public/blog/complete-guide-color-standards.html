The Complete Guide to Color Standards: CSS, RAL, Pantone and NCS

Published 2025-02-15 · Coloralyze

There are dozens of color standards in use today, but four dominate across design, industry and digital work: CSS Named Colors, RAL, Pantone and NCS. This guide explains each one and helps you choose the right tool for your project.

1. CSS Named Colors — The web standard

CSS Named Colors are a set of 148 color keywords defined by the W3C (World Wide Web Consortium) and supported by every modern browser. Examples include dodgerblue, tomato, rebeccapurple and papayawhip.

Use when: You are writing HTML or CSS and want human-readable color names instead of HEX codes. They improve code readability and are universally recognized by browsers.

Limitation: Only 148 colors — far fewer than Pantone or NCS. They were not designed for physical reproduction.

2. RAL Classic — The industrial standard

RAL Classic was created in Germany in 1927 and has grown to 213 standardized colors used in paints, coatings, plastics and signage throughout Europe. Each color has a 4-digit code (RAL 3000 Flame Red, RAL 7016 Anthracite Grey).

Use when: Ordering industrial paints, powder coatings, vehicle finishes or construction materials. RAL Classic codes are universally understood by European manufacturers and coating suppliers.

Limitation: Only 213 colors — limited palette for creative work. HEX approximations exist but physical colors must be verified with a RAL fan deck.

→ Browse RAL Classic colors

3. RAL Design System+ — The architectural extension

RAL Design System+ expanded the RAL system to over 1,625 colors organized by hue angle, lightness and chroma. The notation (e.g. RAL 220 50 40) is systematic and perceptually uniform.

Use when: Specifying architectural elements, interior surfaces or product designs where a wide, systematically organized color space is needed. Popular in the Netherlands, Germany and Scandinavia.

→ Browse RAL Design colors

4. Pantone — The global brand standard

Pantone is the most internationally recognized color system, with over 1,867 Solid Coated colors plus extensive ranges for fashion, home and digital. Pantone codes (e.g. Pantone 186 C) are used in brand guidelines, packaging and fashion worldwide.

Use when: Defining brand colors, specifying print materials, working in fashion or packaging. Pantone is understood by printers and manufacturers on every continent.

Limitation: Pantone requires licensing for full access to their matching systems. HEX approximations are available but physical fan decks are required for accurate print work.

→ Browse Pantone colors

5. NCS — The perception standard

The Natural Colour System describes colors as humans perceive them using blackness, chromaticness and hue angle. It contains 1,950 colors and is the official standard in Sweden, Norway and Spain.

Use when: Specifying interior colors, wall paints or surface materials where perceptual consistency under different lighting conditions matters. NCS is the language of Scandinavian design and interior architecture.

→ Browse NCS colors

Quick comparison

For web development: CSS Named Colors

For industrial coatings and signage: RAL Classic

For architectural and interior design: RAL Design or NCS

For brand identity and print: Pantone

For interior design with perceptual accuracy: NCS
