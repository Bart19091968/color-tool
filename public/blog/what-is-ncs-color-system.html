What is the NCS Color System and How is it Used in Interior Design?

Published 2025-01-25 · Coloralyze

The Natural Colour System (NCS) is one of the most scientifically rigorous color standards in existence — and yet it is one of the least known outside of Scandinavia and professional design circles. This guide explains what it is, how to read it and why designers love it.

What is NCS?

NCS was developed by the Scandinavian Colour Institute in Stockholm, Sweden, starting in the 1960s. It is based on research by Ewald Hering into how the human visual system perceives color, specifically the idea that we see color as a mixture of six elementary colors: White, Black, Yellow, Red, Blue and Green.

Unlike Pantone (which defines physical inks) or RAL (which defines industrial coatings), NCS describes how a color looks to the human eye. This makes it remarkably consistent across different lighting conditions and materials.

NCS is the official standard in Sweden, Norway and Spain and is widely used in South Africa, Australia and throughout Europe.

How to read NCS notation

An NCS code looks like this: NCS S 2030-R90B

  • NCS — Natural Colour System
  • S — Second Edition (the current standard)
  • 20 — Blackness: 20% black content (0 = pure white, 100 = pure black)
  • 30 — Chromaticness: 30% color saturation (0 = grey, 100 = maximum vividness)
  • R90B — Hue: Red, leaning 90% towards Blue (effectively a blue-violet)

The four hue letters are Yellow, Red, Blue and Green. A color between two hues is written as e.g. Y30R (Yellow leaning 30% towards Red — an orange-yellow).

Neutral greys have no hue component: NCS S 5000-N is a medium grey with 50% blackness and 0% chromaticness.

Why interior designers prefer NCS

Interior designers and architects choose NCS for three main reasons:

1. Predictability under different lighting. Because NCS describes the perceived color rather than a pigment formula, it behaves consistently as lighting changes. A wall specified as NCS S 2030-Y50R will look the same warm orange-red in daylight as in LED light.

2. Easy navigation. The systematic notation means you can easily find adjacent colors. Increase blackness by 10 for a darker version, decrease chromaticness for a more muted variant.

3. Wide material application. NCS is used for paint, fabric, plastic, glass and more. Specifying NCS ensures all materials in a project match visually, regardless of material.

NCS vs Pantone vs RAL

Use NCS for interior design, architecture and any project where perceptual consistency across materials matters. Use Pantone for print, branding and fashion. Use RAL for industrial coatings, powder coats and signage.
